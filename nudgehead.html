Enhancing welfare without a theory of welfare
DANIEL M HAUSMAN October 2019

How do we determine what promotes wellbeing?! Who is the judge? How can we tell which choices are "better" than others? There is no way to know!

Background: As marketers have known for millenia and BS has recently established, satisfying people's preferences does not equal enhancing their wellbeing.

➢ To get around this problem of welfare evaluation, BSist Robert Sugden proposes that number of opportunities should replace wellbeing as a criterion to appraise institutions and policies. [alert! alert! standard economist in disguise!]

In Sugden's view, behavioral economists have conjured up fictitious inner rational agents, whose preferences paternalistic policies satisfy. The case for paternalism collapses without these ghosts, ready to approve of the wise and benevolent policies behavioral economists endorse. [...] There is no way to tell whether in fluencing their choices might benefit them and no justification for paternalism, whether coercive or 'libertarian'.Sugden counts the author of this paper among the ranks of deluded BSers!

➢ Hausman, after much grumbling, agrees with Sugden's 2018 thesis, but he also points out that economists need some way to measure opportunities, which cannot be done without an appraisal of the values of the alternatives- AKA paternalism. This is an issue that Sugden's current work avoids, although he was an early and influential contributor to this very debate in 1982.

Middle Part:

  • Hausman acknowledges that preferences are an unreliable guide to wellbeing, and suggests that this can be mitigated by accepting the weak premise that they are a little more reliable when the subject is both competent and self-interested.
  • But also, there is no well-established philosophical theory of well-being that economists can rely on. Assuming that satisfying preferences = wellbeing naturally results in any outcome = wellbeing. This is useless.
  • He lost! Hausman admits that at this time, he advises a reliance on "truisms," "folk theory," or "commonsensical value judgments" to determine what things make life better.

Conclusion:

➢ “Behavioral economics, conjoined with the folk theory of well-being and an everyday understanding of deliberation, provides guidance concerning when to regard preferences as reliable indicators of wellbeing, how to elicit less distorted preferences and how to correct for the distortions caused by false beliefs and deliberative foibles.” Basically just use your brain.
