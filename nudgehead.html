Enhancing welfare without a theory of welfare
Daniel M. Hausman

How do we determine what promotes wellbeing?! Who is the judge? How can we tell which choices are "better" than others? There is no way to know!

Background: As marketers have known for millenia and economists are just beginning to accept, satisfying people's preferences does not equate to enhancing their wellbeing.

To get around this problem of welfare evaluation, BSist Robert Sugden proposes that number of opportunities should replace wellbeing as a criterion to appraise institutions and policies. [alert! alert! standard economist in disguise!] In Sugden's view, behavioral economists have conjured up fictitious inner rational agents, whose preferences paternalistic policies satisfy. The case for paternalism collapses without these ghosts, ready to approve of the wise and benevolent policies behavioral economists endorse. [...] There is no way to tell whether in fluencing their choices might benefit them and no justification for paternalism, whether coercive or 'libertarian'.Sugden counts the author of -gasp- this paper among the ranks of deluded BSers! What drama! But it doesn't seem to occur to anyone that nudging only works when people don't have a strong desire for the other thing, so the whole paternalism debate is a little... inconsequential.
