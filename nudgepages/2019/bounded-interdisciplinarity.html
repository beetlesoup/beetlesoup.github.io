Bounded interdisciplinarity: critical interdisciplinary perspectives on context and evidence in behavioural public policies
JORAM FEITSMA, MARK WHITEHEAD 24 September 2019

Known for its ‘geniune interdisciplinarity’, this paper argues that BPP is actually a convenient fusing of economics with certain branches of psychology, whose pronounced lack of engagement with a broad range of relevant social sciences limits the capacity for comprehensiveness and disruptiveness that truly interdisciplinary work is capable of.

Middle Part:

  • The rationalist advocacy of RCTs and the straightforward moving from problem definition to implementation has been widely contested in Evidence-Based Policy literature, especially when applied to "wicked problems" - this only produces evidence of what already worked in specific contexts, with limited, inconclusive evidence of the impact of the policy solution itself.
  • Critical public administration scholars studying evidence use have repeatedly pointed out that policy ‘facts’ do not speak for themselves, but become authorized as ‘evidence’ through policymakers' fabrication, cherry-picking, or disputing evidence.
  • BPP is continuously reproducing its own disciplinary assumptions while disempowering others. This may lead to an over-psychologized policy process in which policy design is narrowly approached from an individualist, cognitive-economic orientation that does not thoroughly explore contexts at the structural level and disregards a whole series of other forms of knowledge (qualitative, local, tacit, and phronetic)
