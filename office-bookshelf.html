Our Long Walk to Economic Freedom
Johan Fourie		 Sub-Saharan Africa has an average annual income of one-seventh of the world average, and the economic history of Africa is generally poorly understood. This book is about understanding why, because we want to see things improve. We want to understand what the roots of Africa's prosperity will be, and what policies could help to achieve that. Anthony Hopkins has identified six schools of thought that characterise the field since its establishment in the 1950s. The first, modernisation theory, transferred 'successful' Western policies without considertion of the history and context particular to each region. In the 1970s, modernisation theory was replaced by the dependency school which suggested that foreign capitalism had created underdevelopment. The policy response was to sever ties with the West. The third school, Marxism, shifted the focus to Africans and their own modes of production, soon running into difficulties as the complexity of African societies that they were uncovering in the archives was at odds with their preconceived ideas. By the 1980s the Annales school, associated with the style of an earlier generation of French historians, attracted interest. Its approach was to focus on long-term social history that included groups usually excluded from conventional history, such as women and children. It also incorporated unconventional topics such as demography, climate and disease. Yet the Annalistes were more 'concerned to understand the world rather than change it'.5 the Annalistes were soon displaced by the postmodernists, the fifth school. But the focus with postmodernism was different: instead of material reality, attention turned to cultural history.
