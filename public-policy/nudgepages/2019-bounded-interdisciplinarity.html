Bounded interdisciplinarity: critical interdisciplinary perspectives on context and evidence in behavioural public policies
JORAM FEITSMA, MARK WHITEHEAD 24 September 2019

Known for its ‘geniune interdisciplinarity’, this paper argues that BPP merely fuses economics with certain branches of psychology, and its lack of engagement with a broad range of relevant social sciences limits the capacity for comprehensiveness and disruptiveness that truly interdisciplinary work is capable of.

Main Points:

➢ The authors' fields of geography and public administration include study of how class and race contribute to decision-making. BPP would benefit from integrating with researchers that adopt open-ended, ethnographic methodology to understand certain problems.

➢ We would see it as valuable if BPP would combine its micro-level psychologist's gaze with the macro-level perspective of the sociologist. Attempting to merge these gazes and their underlying paradigmatic views would undoubtedly raise conflict, from which necessary perspectives we hope will arise.

Criticisms of [Not All] BPP:

  • RCTs are expensive and short-sighted: The rationalist advocacy of RCTs and the straightforward moving from problem definition to implementation has been widely contested in Evidence-Based Policy literature, especially when applied to "wicked problems" - this only produces evidence of what already worked in specific contexts, with limited, inconclusive evidence of the impact of the policy solution itself.
  • Dishonesty: Public administration scholars have repeatedly pointed out that policy facts care about are brought about by policymakers' feelings fabrication, cherry-picking, or disputing evidence.
  • OneTwo-Track Mind: BPP is continuously reproducing its own disciplinary assumptions while disempowering others, often approaching policy design from an individualist, cognitive-economic orientation that disregards a whole series of other forms of knowledge (qualitative, local, tacit, and phronetic).

Example: it seems that the famous opt-in vs opt-out organ donor study should now be called 'infamous' - a follow up study shows a decrease in actual donations due to the grieving families' refusal, for which they were able to give the reason: "how did you know they wanted it and didn't just not see the box?"

The paper claims that an uncited copycat study in Spain performed the opt-out nudge successfully, due to the fact that they also studied family-related barriers and improved aspects of the donation process.

BPP examples that offer interdisciplinary ways of gathering and valuing evidence:

  • Dutch Behavioural Insights Team for Infrastructure and Water Management
  • The US Government's Social and Behavioural Sciences Team's approach to understanding Flint's water problem
  • Behavioural Insights Team UK's Behavioural Government report, 2018

Admittedly, [BIT UK's] 'Behavioural Government [report]' does acknowledge the role of wider structures at several points, stating that 'reforms cannot focus on individuals in isolation - they also need to consider how systems, processes and institutions create behaviours.' However, this line of thought has not informed the report in a deep sense.
