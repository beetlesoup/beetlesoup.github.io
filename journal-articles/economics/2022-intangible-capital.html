The Economics of Intangible Capital (Link)
NICOLAS CROUZET, JANICE C EBERLY, ANDREA L EISFELDT, DIMITRIS PAPANIKOLAOU on Journal of Economic Perspectives (American Economic Association)Summer 2022

[INCOMPLETE]Intangibles often amount to "missing" or "mismeasured" capital. This study chooses a more affirmative description [productive capital that lacks a physical presence and requires a storage medium] in order to discuss the two implications of non-rivalry in use and limited excludability.

  • The degree of non-rivalry in use within the firm can be thought of as a parameter ranging from 0 to 1. At one extreme, a luxury brand cannot be used to market household cleaning products. At the other, payroll software can be used across the entire firm, as can a healthy corporate culture. In that case, investing in intangibles may generate economies of scale or scope.

Intangible has accumulation (need for investment), depreciation, and firm-specificity in common with physical capital.
