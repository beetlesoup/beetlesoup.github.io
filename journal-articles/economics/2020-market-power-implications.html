The Rise of Market Power and the Macroeconomic Implications (Link)
JAN DE LOECKER, JAN EECKHOUT, GABRIEL UNGER on The Quarterly Journal of Economics23 January 2020

[USA] Based on firm-level data, aggregate markups were more or less stable between 1955 and 1980 with a steady rise since 1980, from 21% above cost to 61% above cost in 2016 - but the median is constant; it is the upper percentiles that have gone up substantially.

The macroeconomic puzzle of the decline of the labor share can be explained by the reallocation of economic activity toward large firms:

As markups increase, firms spend less on labor. When enough large firms reduce their expenditure on labor, it translates into an aggregate decline in the labor share.

Rising markups has a role in the decrease of capital share, the decrease in low-skilled wages, the decrease in labor market participation, and the decrease in labor reallocation and in interstate migration.

This paper:

  • cites many other studies with whom their findings are consistent and discusses how they are different
